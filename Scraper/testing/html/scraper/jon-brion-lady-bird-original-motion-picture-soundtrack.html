Lady Bird (Original Motion Picture Soundtrack)

  • Lakeshore
  • Fire
\xe2\x80\xa2 2017
7.3
  • by Sasha Geffen
    Contributor
  • Rock
January 4 2018

Using piano, strings, a wind ensemble, and light percussion, Brion\xe2\x80\x99s score subtly and deftly mirrors the existential ambling of Greta Gerwig\xe2\x80\x99s outstanding coming-of-age film.

\n

Jon Brion has probably never been a 17-year-old girl, and yet there\xe2\x80\x99s something to the loose, swinging compositions he\xe2\x80\x99s put together for Greta Gerwig\xe2\x80\x99s directorial debut Lady Bird that indicates a grasp of what it\xe2\x80\x99s like to be young, seething, and entirely dissatisfied with your surroundings. Gerwig\xe2\x80\x99s heroine, whose name is Christine but who goes by Lady Bird as a bid for an identity beyond the material conditions of her lower-middle-class family in Sacramento, Calif., introduces herself within the first minutes of the film by flinging herself out of a moving car during an argument with her mother about why she must go to college on the East Coast. You see her mom scream and then there\xe2\x80\x99s a cut to a pink cast with the words \xe2\x80\x9cFUCK YOU MOM\xe2\x80\x9d firmly scrawled on it. It\xe2\x80\x99s quite an entrance, and a movie with a heavier hand might have thrown something punk-rebellious in the soundtrack, or at the very least something with a snare-driven backbeat and an electric guitar. Not Lady Bird. Christine\xe2\x80\x99s not punk; she\'s just exasperated and understimulated, searching tirelessly for a way out of the cage she sees around her life.

\n

In the past decade or so, movies about quirky high schoolers have thankfully aged out of their twee phase. Lady Bird has far more in common with Kenneth Lonergan\xe2\x80\x99s 2011 stunner Margaret than it does with Juno; it has gravitas, and it assigns more meaning to the looking than the finding, the neat endings, the easy answers. With a piano, string, wind ensemble, and light percussion, Brion\xe2\x80\x99s score deftly mirrors Lady Bird\xe2\x80\x99s existential ambling. His notes barely glance off the piano keys, and there always seems to be a spring in the music\xe2\x80\x99s step, leaving just enough room for the film\xe2\x80\x99s actors to carry the real weight. This isn\xe2\x80\x99t the kind of soundtrack that tells you exactly how to feel; Lady Bird isn\xe2\x80\x99t that kind of movie. Its magic happens in the space between the characters, the tensions and betrayals and bruises they inflict on each other and how they propel each other forward. There\xe2\x80\x99s an arc to the film\xe2\x80\x94it doesn\xe2\x80\x99t just throw its characters in a spin-cycle of increasing absurdity, like the Brion-scored I Heart Huckabees\xe2\x80\x94but it\xe2\x80\x99s a subtle, emotional one. Lady Bird\xe2\x80\x99s big quest is just to fly across the country to college in New York without destroying every meaningful relationship she\xe2\x80\x99s ever had along the way.

\n

Lady Bird is one of Brion\xe2\x80\x99s more unassuming scores (you wouldn\xe2\x80\x99t necessarily be able to place its theme instantaneously if you heard it out in the wild), but it\xe2\x80\x99s also one of his least cynical. Even the gut-mangling Charlie Kaufman movie Synecdoche, New York had its sneering moments with wildly sarcastic Brion themes to accompany them. Lady Bird\xe2\x80\x99s score stays in a consistent mode, never pushing beyond a light touch, which is fitting enough for a film set in Sacramento, a place whose anodyne pleasantness threatens to swallow its heroine alive. And still in all the sunniness lurks the sense that something is happening. Something is pushing Christine toward a precipice that she\xe2\x80\x99s both terrified and eager to scale. A bass drum pounds insistently on \xe2\x80\x9cMaybe\xe2\x80\x9d; piano keys strike up through the woodwinds like mountains through fog on \xe2\x80\x9cLeaving\xe2\x80\x9d; there\xe2\x80\x99s a track called \xe2\x80\x9cHope\xe2\x80\x9d and a track called \xe2\x80\x9cMore Hope\xe2\x80\x9d and one \xe2\x80\x9cHope Against Hope\xe2\x80\x9d and then, finally, the minor key conclusion, \xe2\x80\x9cHope?\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Set in the early 2000s, right around the time Jon Brion started composing for directors like Paul Thomas Anderson who prefer to see the world askew, Lady Bird easily could have aimed for millennial nostalgia with its music cues. Rather than have Christine listen to the American Analog Set or early Sufjan Stevens, though, Gerwig pivots her emotional growth around a ubiquitous Dave Matthews Band single. Brion follows her lead. The way he colors the film\xe2\x80\x99s world leaves it wide open, with room enough for gentle movement and subtle triumphs. His light, playful motifs buoy the narrative without crowding it, and help paint a rich, fulfilling portrait of a girl on the edge, ready to fly.
