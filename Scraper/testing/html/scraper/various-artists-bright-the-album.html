Bright: The Album

  • Atlantic
\xe2\x80\xa2 2017
3.2
  • by Evan Rytlewski
    Contributor
January 5 2018

None of the wild imagination fueling David Ayers\xe2\x80\x99 bloated orc cop epic carries over to its soundtrack, which feels test-marketed to a fault.

\n

Of 2017\xe2\x80\x99s many examples of how indiscriminate media companies have become in their race for streaming content, none was more conspicuous than Netflix\xe2\x80\x99s Will Smith vehicle Bright, a $90 million eyesore with the budget of a blockbuster but the heart of a Syfy network original. Imagining a fantasy Los Angeles where humans coexist, uneasily, with mythological creatures, it\xe2\x80\x99s the setup for some horrifically bungled social commentary, and a movie so unlovable that even Netflix\xe2\x80\x99s own social media team couldn\xe2\x80\x99t resist taking potshots. Yes, it is as bad as the critics say. And no, it isn\xe2\x80\x99t worth a hate watch.

\n

Thanks to Netflix\xe2\x80\x99s closely guarded ratings metrics, there\xe2\x80\x99s no real knowing how many subscribers actually streamed David Ayers\xe2\x80\x99 orc cop epic, but all available evidence suggests the film wasn\xe2\x80\x99t nearly the cultural event imagined by its soundtrack, which is every bit as gaudy and grandiose as the movie that spawned it. Built from the same mishmash of toothless pop rap, bloated EDM, and battle-montage alt-rock as the soundtrack to Ayers\xe2\x80\x99 previous movie, Suicide Squad, it exists in an alternate universe where Bright became the box-office Goliath its creators conceived it as. For its imagined audience of Bright obsessives, a pair of heavy-handed opening tracks dutifully conjures the gritty, blighted world of the movie: Logic and Rag\xe2\x80\x98n\xe2\x80\x99Bone Man\xe2\x80\x99s brooding \xe2\x80\x9cBroken People\xe2\x80\x9d and the weepy Bastille anthem \xe2\x80\x9cWorld Gone Mad.\xe2\x80\x9d Like the film, the album takes itself very, very seriously.

\n

Imagine Dragons aren\xe2\x80\x99t actually on the soundtrack, but they loom so large over it that they deserve an honorary executive producer credit. The album\xe2\x80\x99s rock tracks are steeped in the band\xe2\x80\x99s tortured bombast, and their dalliances with other genres provide the model for its seemingly arbitrary crossover pairings. EDM producers Steve Aoki and Marshmello partner with Lil Uzi Vert and Migos, respectively, while Imagine Dragons imitators X Ambassadors share a track with Machine Gun Kelly and Bebe Rexha, one of the many industry bench players peppering an album devoted to the latest, glossiest Top 40 sounds.

\n

The soundtrack\xe2\x80\x99s one true standout is one of its most unassuming songs. Meek Mill, YG, and Snoop Dogg\xe2\x80\x99s fiery East Coast/West Coast team-up \xe2\x80\x9cThat\xe2\x80\x99s My Nigga\xe2\x80\x9d may be less high concept than the deeply silly DRAM/Neil Young head-scratcher \xe2\x80\x9cCampfire,\xe2\x80\x9d but it has the advantage of performers who have not only chemistry but also genuine interest in each other\xe2\x80\x99s worlds. The same can\xe2\x80\x99t be said of the album\xe2\x80\x99s other collaborations, including the turgid A$AP Rocky/Tom Morello coupling \xe2\x80\x9cFTW (Fuck the World),\xe2\x80\x9d which imagines how much more awkward the Prophets of Rage album could have been if it made any effort to reach out to a younger audience.

\n

One of the few things the movie Bright has going for it, despite its grim execution, is conviction. There\xe2\x80\x99s the seed of a genuine passion project buried beneath the movie\xe2\x80\x99s hideous makeup, crude racial caricatures, and endless shootouts. None of that wild imagination carries over to its soundtrack. Save for one inexplicable Neil Young cameo, it\xe2\x80\x99s test-marketed to a fault, coasting on popular sounds, top-dollar production, and proven star power. In that regard, though, these songs are just like the movie itself. They openly long to be hits, but they just aren\xe2\x80\x99t any good.
