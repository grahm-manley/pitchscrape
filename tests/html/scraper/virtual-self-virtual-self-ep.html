Virtual Self EP

  • Self-released
\xe2\x80\xa2 2018
5.9
  • by Philip Sherburne
    Contributing Editor
  • Electronic
January 3 2018

Adopting a new alias, the EDM producer Porter Robinson sets aside Worlds\xe2\x80\x99 electro-pop crossover and embraces early-2000s trance and video-game music.

\n

Featured Tracks:

\xe2\x80\x9cGhost Voices\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x94 Virtual SelfVia\xc2\xa0SoundCloud

Porter Robinson has spent much of his career trying to escape his own shadow. The North Carolina native released his first record, a pounding trance anthem, when he was a 15-year-old denizen of online production forums, and his career since then has followed a zigzag path away from the cruder hallmarks of youthful ebullience. The brash, buzzing electro-house and dubstep of his 2011 debut for Skrillex\xe2\x80\x99s OWSLA label gave way to the more plangent melodies of \xe2\x80\x9cLanguage\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cEasy\xe2\x80\x9d; his 2014 album Worlds largely jettisoned rave bombast in favor of wistful electro pop and Vocaloid balladry. In early 2017, he explicitly disavowed much of his own work, winnowing down his catalog to a slim list of keepers: \xe2\x80\x9ci\xe2\x80\x99ve been making music for 12 years and i only wrote 11 songs, wow.\xe2\x80\x9d He was 24 years old at the time.

\n

These sorts of evasive maneuvers may be increasingly familiar to digital natives eager to divert attention from early traces of their public selves. But with his latest project, Robinson not only changes course once again; he also re-engages with the sounds of his youth. The debut EP from his Virtual Self alias makes a headlong plunge into an aesthetic rooted in the late 1990s and early 2000s: hard trance and Eurodance crosscut with sparkling keygen melodies and the high-velocity soundtracks of video games like \xe2\x80\x9cDance Dance Revolution\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cBeatmania IIDX.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

The EP\xe2\x80\x99s neon onslaught couldn\xe2\x80\x99t be further from Worlds\xe2\x80\x99 delicate textures and crossover inclinations, which found common cause with electro-pop acts like Chvrches and M83. \xe2\x80\x9cParticle Arts\xe2\x80\x9d is a 175-BPM juggernaut of trance stabs and happy-hardcore breaks; \xe2\x80\x9cEON BREAK\xe2\x80\x9d deals in hardstyle cadences and machine-gun snares. Where Worlds\xe2\x80\x99 aggressive moments were tempered by wispy atmospheres, Virtual Self opts for a more unvarnished palette, and its funkless, hyperactive rhythms are evocative of \xe2\x80\x9cDDR\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x99s relentless acrobatics.

\n

Not everything is a copy of vintage sounds. Despite the rushing snare rolls and classic \xe2\x80\x9cReese\xe2\x80\x9d bass of \xe2\x80\x9cGhost Voices,\xe2\x80\x9d the song\xe2\x80\x99s lithe vocal flips and slinky air peg it to the post-Disclosure era; it\xe2\x80\x99s the record\xe2\x80\x99s most contemporary-sounding cut. And the carefully sculpted vocal synths of \xe2\x80\x9ca.i.ngel (Become God)\xe2\x80\x9d aren\xe2\x80\x99t terribly far off from the sound of Oneohtrix Point Never\xe2\x80\x99s Garden of Delete. There are other similarities with that album, too: Just like digital breadcrumbs that OPN used to construct Garden of Delete\xe2\x80\x99s elaborate backstory, Robinson created an array of Twitter accounts to lend mystery to the Virtual Self rollout. Much like OPN\xe2\x80\x99s invented genre of \xe2\x80\x9chypergrunge,\xe2\x80\x9d Virtual Self\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cneotrance\xe2\x80\x9d is a way of tackling ideas of nostalgia, adolescence, and, crucially, the contingent nature and arbitrary standards of taste.

\n

In its saccharine overdrive, much of the EP is distinctly, unabashedly unfashionable\xe2\x80\x94corny, even. Unlike someone like Lone, whose rave revivalism views its inspirations through tastefully rose-tinted lenses, Virtual Self hones in on the most garish aspects of Robinson\xe2\x80\x99s influences, burning away any possibility of subtlety beneath a billion-watt gleam. Halfway through \xe2\x80\x9cKey,\xe2\x80\x9d he even indulges in an honest-to-goodness key change\xe2\x80\x94pop music\xe2\x80\x99s hokiest trope.

\n

A number of self-consciously underground artists have been playing with similar ideas for a while; Lorenzo Senni and Evian Christ have both tackled trance from a position of tongue-in-cheek plausible deniability. Robinson, though, is known and loved for his sincerity and his sensitivity, which makes the Virtual Self project feel different\xe2\x80\x94more like an embrace, rather than a nudge and a wink. As it happens, December 31, 2017 marked a remarkable historical footnote: On that day, every living adult had been born in the 20th century; everyone not yet 18 had been born in the 21st. The notion of a generational divide couldn\xe2\x80\x99t be rendered in starker terms. No matter which side you were born on, Virtual Self\xe2\x80\x99s trance revamp reminds us that the boundaries of taste are always in flux\xe2\x80\x94and that ultimately, nostalgia will rehabilitate even the shaggiest underdogs.
