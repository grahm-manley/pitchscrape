Water Memory

  • Self-released
\xe2\x80\xa2 2017
7.6
  • by Philip Sherburne
    Contributing Editor
  • Electronic
January 6 2018

Across its 40 minutes, the Los Angeles artist builds a modular synth album that is enveloping and cohesive. What initially sounds static may, upon closer examination, reveal a wealth of movement.

\n

There\xe2\x80\x99s a joke that synthesizer fanatics ruefully share: What\xe2\x80\x99s the best way to get a musician to stop making records? Get them hooked on modular synths. Part building block, part bar puzzle, modular synths are small, often arcane electronic gizmos that, when chained together, are capable of creating soundscapes of incredible complexity. They\xe2\x80\x99re notorious for both their steep learning curve and their status as fetish objects, hence the joke: Synth heads can get so lost in trying to sculpt the perfect waveform, they forget how to write actual songs. But making music on a modular synthesizer can be so absorbing, the act of creation is its own reward. The process eclipses the need for a final product.

\n

You get that sense from watching the videos that document Emily A. Sprague\xe2\x80\x99s own evolving modular setup. Take a clip like \xe2\x80\x9cModular \xe2\x80\x98Piano\xe2\x80\x99 Music ~ Eurorack Ambient\xe2\x80\x9d: A metal box brimming with knobs, tangled wires, and multi-colored LEDs sits on a desk, spilling forth a soft, burbling sequence of tones. The sound is too organized to scan as totally random, but it\xe2\x80\x99s too random to be properly composed. Every now and then, Sprague briefly enters the frame to twist a knob, though the effect on the sound is often imperceptible. The sound (the \xe2\x80\x9cpatch\xe2\x80\x9d) could conceivably keep doing what it\xe2\x80\x99s doing until the power grid goes down, and it\xe2\x80\x99s so pleasant you could listen for almost as long. Like Eno said of ambient music, it\xe2\x80\x99s the perfect combination of ignorable and interesting, and much of its generative magic would be lost in the translation to a finished recording.

\n

Fortunately, after a year\xe2\x80\x99s worth of exploratory videos and SoundCloud sketches, Sprague, who recently relocated from New York to Los Angeles, finally steps away from the workbench bearing her debut solo synthesizer album. It was worth the wait. Water Memory is more than just a collection of experiments; its 40-minute run is both enveloping and cohesive, with all five tracks building off one another, each developing upon the album\xe2\x80\x99s pastoral and aquatic themes. Sprague\xe2\x80\x99s compositional style stakes out a middle ground between approaches adopted by her peers. It\xe2\x80\x99s not as melodically or rhythmically active as someone like Qasim Naqvi, yet it\xe2\x80\x99s not as austere as Sarah Davachi. What initially sounds static may, upon closer examination, reveal a wealth of movement. At first, the opening \xe2\x80\x9cA Lake\xe2\x80\x9d sounds like a single held chord. But as you ease into its 13-minute expanse, its elements gradually reveal themselves: Slowly moving bass tones underpin a more mercurial middle layer, and on top, the dazzling high-end dances like the surface of a sunlit body of water.

\n

She\xe2\x80\x99s not always so minimalist. \xe2\x80\x9cWater Memory 1,\xe2\x80\x9d perhaps the album\xe2\x80\x99s most engaging track, coasts atop a gently cycling sequence of tones, shifting subtly all the while. The short, wistful \xe2\x80\x9cDock\xe2\x80\x9d ruminates on a set of tone clusters with a harmonium-like sound, making gingerly stabs at melody. The closing \xe2\x80\x9cYour Pond,\xe2\x80\x9d the album\xe2\x80\x99s emotional highlight, also hovers in the balance between repetition and actual song form, with a melody that moves like creeping vines, reaching out and then doubling back upon itself. The nine-minute \xe2\x80\x9cWater Memory 2\xe2\x80\x9d comes closest to resembling her exploratory videos, a clear descendant of Eno\xe2\x80\x99s aleatory approach to setting up a handful of variables and then stepping out of the way.

\n

Throughout, the outward simplicity of the music masks the deep complexity of her sounds. It took me a dozen or more listens to realize that the cadence of \xe2\x80\x9cWater Memory 1\xe2\x80\x9d is both relatively straightforward and rather strange. Parsed one way, it\xe2\x80\x99s a standard eight-note progression, but once you hone in on its particulars, it sways with an uneven sense of motion, like a pendulum on a planet with two suns.

\n

As it happens, in addition to her synthesizer music and her work producing and recording other musicians in her own studio, Sprague also plays in the indie-folk group Florist, whose acoustic instrumentation and hushed vocals couldn\xe2\x80\x99t seem further from her solo electronic endeavors. Her writing there has tackled some big ideas\xe2\x80\x94a brush with death, an extended convalescence, the loss of her mother\xe2\x80\x94with understated grace. Here, she sets aside those existential themes, but one constant carries over: her eye for detail. Whereas in Florist, it is attuned to trenchant imagery (\xe2\x80\x9cWhat is love if not violet/A beam of light on an autumn afternoon\xe2\x80\x9d), here it refocuses on the surface of sound itself. It is hypnotic to behold.
